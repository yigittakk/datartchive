"Five archives. Whose artists make it in?"

We queried 77,956 records from five institutions. But not all records are equal — and not all institutions expose their data the same way.

About this project

What we set out to find

This project investigates the persons documented across five active PHAROS member institutions through four research questions: the distribution across institutions, nationality, gender representation over time, and geographic mobility. Data was queried from artresearch.net via SPARQL using CIDOC-CRM predicates and enriched with biographical records from Getty ULAN.

Methodological note: At the time of data collection, person records were retrievable from five institutions via the artresearch.net SPARQL endpoint. The remaining eight PHAROS members (Courtauld, RKD, NGA, Yale, Hertziana, KHI, Dumbarton Oaks, I Tatti) returned no results — this may reflect differences in data modelling, endpoint availability, or ongoing data integration. Additionally, Wikidata enrichment was planned but unavailable due to an active WDQS outage at time of submission.

01

How are persons distributed across five PHAROS member institutions?

Persons retrieved via CIDOC-CRM SPARQL — four different modelling patterns across institutions. Only verified persons with external identifiers included.

Finding: Bildarchiv Foto Marburg dominates in raw numbers. The highest overlap ratio has shown in the Warburg Institute because 75% of its verified persons are shared with other institutions. The Paul Mellon Centre, Frick Art Research Library, and MIDAS have more verified unique persons than shared.
Limitation: These numbers only reflect persons retrievable via SPARQL from each institution, not their actual collection size. These figures are not directly comparable across institutions due to differences in data modelling, URI patterns, and external identifier coverage.

Which nationalities are represented — and which dominate?

Nationality from Getty ULAN gvp:nationalityPreferred. Only the 24,950 persons linked to Getty ULAN are included — 32% of total records.

Finding: Italian artists lead with 5,653 persons, followed by German and British. This distribution might reflect the institutional composition of PHAROS — particularly Fondazione Zeri and Frick's Italian holdings — rather than a neutral cross-section of art history.

03

How is gender represented — and has it changed across centuries?

Gender from Getty ULAN biographical records (AAT 300189559 = male, 300189557 = female). Active period start year used as decade proxy.

Finding: 93% of verified persons are male artists. Female representation rises from the 1840s, reaching a peak share near 11% in the 1890s before declining. This may reflect both historical barriers and systematic gaps in archival documentation.

04

Where were persons born and where did they die?

Birth and death places from Getty ULAN TGN-linked records via schema:birthPlace and schema:deathPlace.

Finding: There are more deaths than births in London and Paris. Thus, we can say that these are the two most attractive cities for artistic careers. Siena and Edinburgh are two exceptional cities with roughly equal birth and death counts.
Coverage note: Birth place data is available for 5,549 persons and death place for 5,375 — but only 3,355 have both places recorded, which is 10% of verified persons. An additional 2,194 have only birth place, and 2,020 only death place. Of those with both, 51.1% moved (died in a different city), 48.9% stayed. The London/Paris asymmetry is not explained by this gap, confirming a real signal rather than coverage bias.

Conclusions

What the data tells us

01

Institution distribution. Foto Marburg and Frick dominate the verified persons. The high overlap ratio of the Warburg Institute reflects active cross-institutional dialogue. Counts are not directly comparable across institutions due to differences in data modelling and SPARQL coverage.

02

Nationality. Italian artists lead the nationalities of the artists. Western Europe dominates the nationalities in the data for the five current PHAROS members.

03

Gender. Female artists account for only 3.4% of verified persons. Their representation rises modestly from the 1840s. This pattern reflects both historical exclusion and archival documentation bias — Getty ULAN may systematically under-represent women.

04

Geographic mobility. London and Paris attracted more artists than their native artists. 51.1% of the 3,355 persons with both birth and death places recorded died in a different city, so there is a significant geographic mobility. The London/Paris dominance in death counts is not explained by coverage gaps. These cities might have functioned as crucial centres for artistic careers.

→

What remains open. Eight PHAROS members returned no queryable data — they may model their data differently, or may not yet expose it through the shared endpoint. As more institutions publish their records, every finding here may shift.
